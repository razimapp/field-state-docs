A football decision engine: The budget proposal

Fieldstate starts as a practical analyst product: replay a real moment, compare decisions, and explain the likely value and risk of alternative actions.

The opportunity scales in stages. The first stage is sellable as a decision-support and presentation tool. Later stages deepen the model, data, and platform moat.

Executive summary

Fieldstate is already more than an idea: it has a working football decision demo, real match examples, counterfactual analysis, and an evidence-led model approach. The first commercial step is not a large AI build; it is a focused sprint that turns the current work into a shareable investor and pilot asset.

What we can show now

A live walkthrough of real match moments, alternative decisions, visual replay, and explainable value/risk outputs.

What funding buys first

A polished feasibility sprint, production delivery, investor preparation, and a clearer route to early pilots.

What later funding unlocks

Broader validation, better data, stronger models, and the path toward a larger football decision-intelligence platform.

Assumptions: figures are indicative planning ranges, subject to delivery scope, data access, commercial rights, investor structure, and agreed founder/operator responsibilities. This is a staged planning brief, not a final legal or investment offer.

What exists now

Fieldstate already has the foundations of a credible demo: real match data, real player-position context where available, pass-chain replay, counterfactual edits, explainable scores, and an evidence page that separates what is proven from what remains gated.

That restraint matters. The product does not need to claim perfect prediction to be valuable. It can make money first as a tool for analysis, storytelling, coaching review, and opposition preparation. A live walkthrough is available on request.

The bigger vision

The long-term opportunity is to become the decision layer for football: a product that helps analysts, coaches, media teams, and data partners understand not only what happened, but which alternatives existed and what each decision was likely to change.

Commercial routes and rights

The same build has different value depending on the rights structure. A founder-led route, a commissioned build, and a full IP assignment are priced differently because they transfer different rights.

Route What the partner gets What stays protected Pricing logic
Founder-led venture Access to the product, roadmap, and founding technical leadership. Core originator position, company terms, and long-term upside. Lower cash requirement than a buyout, structured through agreed founder and equity terms.
Funded feasibility sprint Demo, evidence pack, technical narrative, investor walkthrough. Underlying IP and future product rights unless separately agreed. Best first step: contained cost, fast proof, clear next decision.
Commissioned MVP Defined deliverable and agreed usage rights. Background know-how, reusable tooling, and future roadmap unless assigned. Priced as a product outcome with agreed usage rights.
Licence Use of the product or outputs for agreed markets, teams, or use cases. Ownership and ability to license elsewhere. Recurring or limited-rights model; attractive if the product proves repeatable.
Full IP assignment Ownership of the code, product concept implementation, docs, and related materials. Little or no retained control unless negotiated. Reflects transfer of the asset itself, not only the work.

Recommended ask

Start with a funded feasibility sprint. It is the fastest, lowest-risk way to turn the current work into an asset investors and partners can evaluate, and every later stage remains available as upside once that asset exists.

Each stage is priced and decided on its own results. That keeps the first commitment small, the milestones clear, and the bigger data and model roadmap open.

The feasibility sprint should include an operational founder-operator retainer of £12.2k/month while active, so production delivery, investor preparation, and commercial follow-up are properly resourced rather than treated as unpaid pre-work.
